Etherscape

Developed for the Sound, Space and Interaction course, Etherscape transforms invisible wireless networks into an immersive sonic experience. The project explores the intersection of sound and spatial interaction by converting network data into audio signals, allowing users to perceive and interact with digital infrastructures in urban environments. Through this sensory translation, Etherscape creates a unique interactive dialogue between users and their surrounding digital landscape, embodying the course's focus on sonic language and spatial interaction beyond traditional screen-based interfaces.

Fragmentation

Created during the "Creative Programming" course, the Fragmentation Tool invites graphic designers to disrupt pixel-perfect workflows with randomness inspired by Dadaism. Users interact with the tool through a drag-and-select mechanism, applying features like displacement, pixelation, and blur to add texture and unpredictability to their designs. This project reflects the course’s focus on programming as a medium for creative expression. It combines algorithmic thinking with a user-friendly interface, reminiscent of design tools like Photoshop, to blend familiarity with experimental possibilities. The tool exemplifies how computational methods can challenge design conventions and open new avenues for creative exploration.

Daiku

Daiku merges the spontaneity of Dadaist practice with the structured elegance of Haiku. It randomly selects phrases from Wikipedia, then enforces 5-7-5 syllable constraints while refining consonant-vowel patterns for phonetic cohesion. Abstract visuals generated via Stable Diffusion complete each piece, blending unpredictability with intentional design choices. Within the Computational Creativity course, Daiku illustrates how systems can emulate and expand human creative processes. Random sampling resonates with Dada’s historical ethos, yet the project’s code-based constraints ensure an authentic haiku form. By foregrounding algorithmic experimentation, Daiku highlights new possibilities for machine-assisted poetry and visual expression.

Designing for Interaction: Strategic Approaches to Increasing Engagement in Interactive Installations

Abstract

The following paper explores how design can aﬀect user engagement and presents three design modification applied to the Proxemics interactive installation as a case study: ambient visual cues, voice-guided instructions, and contextual information. These modifications were designed for improving the interaction experience. Whereas each approach had diﬀerent in- fluences, contextual information was most eﬀective in reducing user hesitation and allowing smoother interaction. The study followed a structured methodology combining qualitative and quantitative approaches to observe how these design changes shaped user behaviour. These findings could contribute to an understanding of how thoughtful design modification can shape and refine user engagement within interactive installations, oﬀering considerations for future work in interactive design.

Enriching Metadata and the Potential Benefit for Colonial Narratives in the Rijksstudio Database

This research, developed during the Digital Heritage course, investigates how enriched metadata in digital museum collections can reshape our understanding of colonial narratives. Through a case study of the Rijksstudio database, the project examines the potential of detailed digital documentation to provide more transparent and nuanced perspectives on historical artifacts. Working at the intersection of cultural preservation and digital innovation, the study explores how technological solutions can help cultural institutions address historical biases and create more inclusive representations of their collections, while considering the ethical implications of digitizing sensitive cultural heritage.

Conditionalism

An exploration of 'conditionalism' as a creative strategy across artistic disciplines, this essay examines how predetermined rules and parameters shape artistic creation. Developed as part of the Strategies in Creative and Performing Arts course, the work analyzes various artists including Marina Abramović, Terry Riley, François Morellet, and Sol LeWitt to demonstrate how systematic approaches to art-making transcend traditional disciplinary boundaries. The research reveals how conditions and parameters can generate diverse artistic outputs while maintaining clear correlations between iterations, contributing to broader discussions about creative methodologies in contemporary art practice.
